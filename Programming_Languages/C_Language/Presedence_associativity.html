Operator Precedence and Associativity in C

What is Operator Precedence?

Operator precedence determines the **order** in which different operators in an expression are evaluated. For example: 

int result = 10 + 5 * 2;

Even though + comes before *, the multiplication happens first because * has higher precedence than +.

What is Associativity?

Associativity defines the **direction** (left-to-right or right-to-left) in which operators of the same precedence are evaluated.

For example, in the expression:

int x = 10 - 5 - 2;

The subtraction is done left to right: (10 - 5) - 2 = 3.

Table of Precedence and Associativity

Here’s a simplified table of C operators, ordered by precedence (highest at top):

Precedence Level Operator Description Associativity
1 () [] . -> Function call, array, member access Left to Right
2 ++ -- + - ! ~ (type) * & sizeof Unary operators Right to Left
3 * / % Multiplication, division, modulus Left to Right
4 + - Addition, subtraction Left to Right
5 << >> Bitwise shift operators Left to Right
6 < <= > >= Relational operators Left to Right
7 == != Equality operators Left to Right
8 & Bitwise AND Left to Right
9 ^ Bitwise XOR Left to Right
10 | Bitwise OR Left to Right
11 && Logical AND Left to Right
12 || Logical OR Left to Right
13 ?: Conditional (Ternary) Right to Left
14 = += -= *= /= etc. Assignment operators Right to Left
15 , Comma operator Left to Right

Example with Precedence

int result = 10 + 20 * 3;

Explanation:

  • Multiplication 20 * 3 happens first → 60
  • Then addition 10 + 60 → 70

Example with Associativity

int x = 100 / 10 * 2;

Explanation: Both / and * have same precedence and left-to-right associativity:

  • 100 / 10 → 10
  • 10 * 2 → 20

Why This is Important

Understanding precedence and associativity helps you write correct expressions and avoid logical errors.

Use () parentheses when in doubt to ensure the desired order of operations. 

int x = (10 + 5) * 3; // Ensures addition happens first
